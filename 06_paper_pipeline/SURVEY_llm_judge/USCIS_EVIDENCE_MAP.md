## NIW (National Interest Waiver) — three prongs

### Prong 1: The proposed endeavour has substantial merit and national importance

| Required evidence | How the survey provides it |
|---|---|
| Field is recognised as important | LLM-as-Judge is foundational to RLHF and AI safety, directly addressed in NIST AI RMF (NIST 2023), EU AI Act (2024), and Executive Order 14110 on Safe, Secure, and Trustworthy AI (2023) |
| Author's work is in the named field | Sole-authored survey at TMLR / ACM CS / JMLR is the canonical "field expertise" exhibit |
| National importance argument | Cite: judging reliability affects (a) consumer AI safety, (b) clinical AI evaluation, (c) federal AI procurement standards, (d) US competitive position in AI |

**Strength of evidence**: Strong. A peer-reviewed survey on a CS-economically-strategic
topic, published in a top-tier free venue, is canonical NIW evidence.

### Prong 2: The applicant is well positioned to advance the proposed endeavour

| Required evidence | How the survey provides it |
|---|---|
| Education + training | (External — your degree) |
| Track record of successes | Sole-authored peer-reviewed survey + companion empirical paper (T02) + technical report (T07 preprint) |
| Plan for future work | The "Open Problems" section (§8) of the survey is itself a research agenda |
| Recognition from others | Citations of the survey (accumulating over 12–36 months); reviewer invitations that follow from a high-profile survey |
| Letters of recommendation | (External — but easier to obtain after a TMLR / JMLR survey) |

**Strength of evidence**: Strong. The survey demonstrates both completed contributions
and a forward-looking research agenda.

### Prong 3: On balance, beneficial to waive the labour cert

| Required evidence | How the survey provides it |
|---|---|
| Impact in the field | Citation count (target 500+) over 24–36 months |
| Difficulty replacing applicant | Sole authorship of a comprehensive survey signals unique authority |
| Speed-to-impact | Free venue publication is faster than visa-cert process |

**Strength of evidence**: Strong if citation target met. Moderate if 100–500 citations.

## EB-1A (Extraordinary Ability) — 10 criteria, need 3+ + final merits

### Criterion (i) — Lesser-known awards / honours
- Not directly addressed by the survey.
- BUT a TMLR Survey Certification badge is a form of recognition.
- Best paper / best survey awards at workshops (NeurIPS workshop, ACL workshop) become available after a high-visibility survey.

**Score**: Indirect support. Not the primary criterion.

### Criterion (ii) — Membership in associations requiring outstanding achievement
- Not directly addressed.
- Note: ACM Senior Member status becomes plausible after several major publications.

**Score**: Not primary.

### Criterion (iii) — Published material about the applicant in major media
- Indirectly supported: a widely-cited survey is itself "published material" cited by future authors.
- The 500+ citation target generates this evidence.
- Direct interviews / press coverage may follow from a high-visibility survey.

**Score**: Indirect. Comes after citation accumulation.

### Criterion (iv) — Service as a judge of the work of others
- After publishing a TMLR survey, **reviewer invitations follow naturally**.
- ICML / NeurIPS / EMNLP / TMLR reviewer roles are then accessible.
- Accumulate 5–10 reviewer invitations over 12–24 months post-publication.
- Each invitation can be cited in petition.

**Score**: Strong, but requires post-publication follow-through. Plan to accept reviewer invitations.

### Criterion (v) — Original scientific or scholarly contributions of major significance
- **Direct, strong**: a comprehensive sole-authored survey is by definition an original scholarly contribution.
- The taxonomy in §3 + the 12 open problems in §8 are quantifiable "original contributions."
- "Major significance" is established by: top-tier venue + citation count + adoption by subsequent papers.

**Score**: Strong (high confidence). The survey is the single strongest exhibit for this criterion.

### Criterion (vi) — Authorship of scholarly articles in professional journals
- **Direct, strongest**: sole-authored peer-reviewed survey at TMLR / ACM CS / JMLR is exactly the type of evidence this criterion requires.
- Quantity multiplier: T02 paper + T07 paper + survey = 3 peer-reviewed publications.
- Quality multiplier: each at a recognised venue.

**Score**: Very strong. This is the single highest-confidence criterion the strategy meets.

### Criterion (vii) — Display of work at artistic exhibitions
- Not applicable to research papers.

### Criterion (viii) — Leading or critical role for distinguished organisations
- Not directly addressed by the survey.
- Could be supported if (post-publication) you take a leadership role in an organisation (e.g., conference workshop chair, journal action editor for TMLR).

**Score**: Indirect; depends on post-publication organisational work.

### Criterion (ix) — High salary or remuneration
- Not directly addressed by the survey.
- A strong publication record can support negotiating higher compensation in subsequent roles.

**Score**: Indirect.

### Criterion (x) — Commercial success in performing arts
- Not applicable.

## Final merits determination

For EB-1A, even after meeting 3+ criteria, USCIS applies a "final merits"
analysis to determine if the applicant is at the very top of the field.

**Final merits evidence the strategy provides**:
1. Citation count of the survey (target 500+ within 3 years)
2. Adoption of the survey's taxonomy by subsequent papers (search Google Scholar for "Author 2027 taxonomy")
3. Companion GitHub repo with community contributions (a public artifact of leadership)
4. Reviewer / editor invitations that follow naturally
5. Speaking invitations to workshops and industry events

The honest assessment: meeting 3 criteria is the easier bar; the final merits
is where solo first-time authors struggle. The 500-citation target directly
addresses this. If achieved, final merits is plausibly met.

## Timeline mapping for petitions

| Petition stage | Earliest realistic filing | Required evidence |
|---|---|---|
| NIW filing | Month 14–18 (post-TMLR publication) | Survey published, T02 published, citation count starting to accumulate |
| EB-1A filing | Month 24–36 (post-substantial citation accumulation) | Survey + T02 + T07 published; citations climbing toward target; reviewer invitations accumulating |

**Honest reality**: An NIW filing at month 14–18 with only the survey just published
is feasible but the impact prong (Prong 3) will be the weakest. Waiting until
month 24 with 50+ citations on the survey would strengthen NIW substantially.

## What NOT to claim in petitions

These would be over-statements that backfire under scrutiny:
- Do NOT claim "international recognition" until citation count crosses 200+
  AND has international citations (not just US authors)
- Do NOT claim "field-defining" — claim "comprehensive" / "definitive reference"
- Do NOT claim "high impact" with <100 citations — let citation count speak for itself
- Do NOT pad the petition with workshop talks / blog posts as primary evidence —
  petitions reward peer-reviewed publications above all

## Companion evidence to assemble alongside the survey

While the survey is in execution, accumulate:

1. **A clean Google Scholar profile**: claim all your publications, verify citations
2. **A clean DBLP profile**: link to all peer-reviewed work
3. **An ORCID record**: for unambiguous attribution
4. **A personal website** with: bio, publications, talks, code
5. **A GitHub profile** with: companion repos for all papers, contribution history
6. **A LinkedIn presence** with: complete academic history, publications listed
7. **Letters of intent** from would-be collaborators / employers (start asking after first publication)

These are part of `13_immigration_evidence/` workflow, not part of the survey
itself. But they are time-sensitive and benefit from being built in parallel.
